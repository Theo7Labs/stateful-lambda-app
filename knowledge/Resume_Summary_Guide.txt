TL;DR: A resume summary is a short, tailored introduction (3–5 lines) at
the top of a resume that highlights your top skills, achievements, and
value to the employer. It replaces generic objectives and helps
recruiters quickly understand why you’re a great fit.

1.  What Is a Resume Summary? A resume summary is a concise opening
    statement that introduces your professional background, key
    achievements, and strengths. It tells the recruiter who you are and
    how you add value to the role. It’s not a list of strengths but a
    snapshot of how your experience benefits the company.

2.  Resume Summary vs. Objective A resume objective states what you
    want. A resume summary shows what you can offer. Career experts
    recommend using a summary—especially for experienced
    candidates—because it demonstrates results, achievements, and
    relevance to the company’s goals.

3.  Why Resume Summaries Matter Your summary captures attention quickly,
    showcases relevant skills, and includes keywords that make your
    resume more ATS-friendly. It also gives immediate proof of your
    value before recruiters read the full document.

4.  How to Write a Strong Summary

-   Write your resume first, then draft your summary.
-   Highlight measurable achievements (e.g., “increased engagement by
    35%”).
-   Ask for feedback from a colleague to identify your top strengths.
-   Keep it brief, specific, and job-focused. Your goal: make the reader
    think, “Tell me more.”

5.  Sample Resume Summaries • Accountant: Results-driven accountant with
    GAAP expertise and a proven track record of optimizing financial
    performance and driving business growth. • Social Media Specialist:
    Creative marketer with 6+ years of experience in developing high-ROI
    campaigns and data-driven content strategies. • Human Resources:
    Strategic HR leader skilled in talent acquisition and employee
    engagement. Known for aligning people strategy with business goals.

Final Takeaway A resume summary is your first impression—make it strong,
clear, and tailored. Focus on how your experience adds value, not just
what you’ve done. Keep it brief, relevant, and written for the job you
want next.
